Thank you for your thoughtful review and constructive questions. Below we provide one-to-one responses to each weakness and question.

W1: FOREST relies on upstream alignment and the effect of noisy supervision is unclear.
A1: We agree that segmentation/matching noise is an important limitation and should have been described more explicitly. In the current pipeline, we apply two safeguards before training FOREST. First, for the single-step setting we discard any event whose post-stow instance count is not exactly pre-stow count + 1. This removes obvious failures from instance segmentation. Second, for the remaining events, we audit the proposed one-to-one correspondence between pre/post instances with Claude-3.7 in a visual-question-answering consistency check over the pre- and post-stow RGB images; there is no manual verification in this step. We did not include a dedicated quantitative noise-ablation, so we do not want to overclaim robustness. Our current evidence is indirect: despite imperfect supervision, FOREST still substantially improves the newly inserted item IoU over heuristics, from 0.3632 to 0.7017 in direct insert and from 0.2167 to 0.6166 in sweep insert, while keeping O-IoU competitive.

W2: The single-step count-change assumption is strong.
A2: We agree. The present paper deliberately studies a restricted but practically important setting: successful single-step stows with exactly one newly inserted item. Under this scope, the count change is used to identify the new item and build slot-aligned supervision. We do not claim that this solves more general rearrangement scenes with multiple simultaneous additions/removals, severe missed detections, or arbitrary occlusion patterns. We see those as natural future extensions. At the same time, the multi-stow rollout results suggest that a useful transition model can still be composed over longer horizons even though training itself is single-step.

W3: The motivation for diffusion versus alternatives is not fully justified.
A3: We agree that the current paper does not prove diffusion is uniquely best for this task, and we should state that more carefully. Our goal is narrower: to test whether a conditional generative model over structured object states can predict non-trivial post-stow rearrangements better than static placement heuristics. The main evidence is empirical and task-specific. In Table 1, FOREST improves N-IoU substantially over copy-paste(+gravity), especially on difficult stows; e.g., on direct-insert surprise cases, N-IoU improves from 0.1034 to 0.5623. In Table 2, using FOREST masks increases DLO MAE by only +0.0016 / +0.0025 relative to ground-truth post-stow masks, which is much smaller than the heuristic gap. We agree that stronger learned baselines would further clarify the benefit of diffusion specifically.

Q1: Please clarify the VLM filtering details.
A4: The VLM is Claude-3.7, used in a visual QA style consistency check over the pre- and post-stow RGB images. Its role is only to audit whether the proposed one-to-one correspondence is visually consistent; it is not part of the FOREST model itself. No manual verification was involved in this filtering step.

Q2: Why these six item attributes, and are they sufficient?
A5: The six attributes (rigid, round, square, conveyable, foldable, fragile) come from ARMBench metadata and were chosen as compact, stow-relevant cues about geometry/material behavior that may affect post-stow outcomes. We do not claim they are exhaustive. Their utility is supported by the appendix ablation: removing the property token lowers direct-insert N-IoU from 0.7017 to 0.6619, and removing both RGB and properties lowers it further to 0.5089. So we view them as useful but partial conditioning signals rather than a complete description of object dynamics.
